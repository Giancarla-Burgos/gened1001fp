Journal of Neuropathological Studies – Vol. 43, No. 2

Neuropathological Implications of Cerebrophagic Syndrome (CPS)

A. L. Mercer1, R. H. Viaro2
1Department of Pathogen Evolution, Caldera Institute
2Neuroinfectious Disease Lab, Hollowbrook Medical Research Center
Published: April 2025 | DOI: 10.1029/jns.2025.042
Abstract: Cerebrophagic Syndrome (CPS) is a novel, rapidly degenerative neuroinfectious disease characterized by aggressive cortical deterioration and memory disintegration. First detected in the outlands of the Novin Province, CPS has demonstrated a unique affinity for limbic structures and appears to be prion-like in transmissibility. This paper documents neuropathological markers, symptom progression, and the socioethical concerns surrounding ongoing containment protocols.
Keywords: Cerebrophagic Syndrome, neurodegeneration, limbic targeting, prion-like transmission, amygdaloid inflammation, containment ethics

1. Introduction

In late 2023, reports emerged from Novin Province of a disease that caused rapid onset dementia-like symptoms and personality destabilization. Initial field diagnosis misattributed the pathology to late-stage rabies or atypical viral encephalitis. However, tissue sampling revealed a distinct pattern of cortical erosion and neurofilament tangling not observed in known neuropathies.

2. Methods

Tissue biopsies were collected post-mortem from 14 confirmed CPS cases. Subjects ranged in age from 22–61. Neuropathological examination included Nissl staining, immunofluorescence tagging, and high-resolution MRI before termination. In addition, 9 live subjects were monitored in containment, with progression tracked through neurocognitive testing.

3. Results

Observations show accelerated deterioration of the hippocampus, amygdala, and anterior cingulate cortex. Subjects exhibited disorganized thought, hyperaggression, and eventual aphasia. One common marker found in all cases was a dense cluster of misfolded protein aggregates—resembling prions—localized in the orbitofrontal region.

Notably, 6 subjects demonstrated the phenomenon of "identity displacement," where self-recognition tests failed but the subjects retained memory of events not personally experienced.

4. Discussion

CPS displays unprecedented spread dynamics via aerosolized vectors in high-exposure zones. Current containment includes Level 4 isolation, though ethical concerns have arisen due to the indefinite status of cognitively aware late-stage patients. The neuroethical dilemma of preserving agency versus preventing transmission remains unresolved.

Molecular modeling suggests the "cerebrophage" may have evolved from synthetic gene-editing vectors designed during the Second Wave. Further research is needed into the origins, as well as into psycho-memetic manifestations observed in survivors.
