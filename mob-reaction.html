The Turning Point

It wasn’t supposed to happen like this. You told yourself it wouldn’t. That people would understand. That they’d see what he did as love.

But when the town found out, it didn’t take long. Not in a place where belief spreads faster than reason. The church bells rang that night, but not for a sermon. Just a signal.

You saw the torchlight first. Then the shouting. By the time you opened the door, they were already on the lawn. A face you recognized — your neighbor, a teacher, someone who once gave your son candy when he got straight As — screamed first.

“He gave it up. For him. For a sickness (lover is part of "hostile group").”

The lover stepped outside. Hands up. Eyes steady. He didn’t run. He just looked at you, like he already knew. Like he forgave you in advance for what came next.

You held your son back. He was screaming. He didn’t stop until the screaming outside did.

And then it was quiet.

The mob left a body and a silence. But they took something else with them too. Maybe your son’s faith. Maybe your own.

He hasn’t spoken since.

And now you have to decide what comes next. He won’t move. Won’t eat. You’ve tried. But tomorrow the transport leaves.
